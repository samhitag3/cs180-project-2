Part 1.2: Finite Difference Operator

In this section, I worked with the cameraman image, and was able to make the edges stand out by binarizing the convolved image after testing multiple threshold values.

First, I convolved the image with the finite difference operators \( D_x \) and \( D_y \) to get \( G_x \) and \( G_y \) respectively, and then I computed the gradient magnitude image, which I got by calculating: \[ \sqrt{G_x^2 + G_y^2} \] Then, after calculating the gradient magnitude image, I experimented with different thresholds, and eventually settled on \( 0.1 \) to create a binarized version of the image, which allowed me to bold the edges while still eliminating most of the noise in the image.

Part 1: Fun with Frequencies!

Part 2.1: Image "Sharpening"

In this section, I used a Gaussian to create a low-pass filter, that when subtracted from the original image, will result in the high frequencies of the image. When adding those high frequencies back to the original image, I get a seemingly sharpened version of the original image. Assume that \( I \) is our original image, \( H \) is the high frequencies of the original image, \( G \) is a Gaussian kernel, and \( \alpha \) is a positive constant: \[ H = I - (I * G) \] \[ I_{\mathrm{sharp}} = I + \alpha H \]

For this entire section, I used a Gaussian kernel with (ksize = 9, sigma = 1).

We can see below how different alpha values can vary the sharpening effect.

As indicated in the spec, I blurred my own image with a Gaussian kernel, and then tried sharpening that.

Part 2.2: Hybrid Images

In this section, I used a Gaussian again to create a low-pass filter for one image, and used the same low-pass filter to get the high frequencies for another image. By averaging the pixel values for the low-frequency and the high-frequency images, I was able to create hybrid images that appear one way close up and another way from far away.

Part 2.3: Gaussian and Laplacian Stacks

In this section, I used Gaussian and Laplacian image stacks to blur images and visualize difference frequencies within images.

I generated the Gaussian stacks by repeatedly convolving the input image with a Gaussian kernel to progressively blur it across multiple levels. I the n made the Laplacian stacks by taking the difference between consecutive Gaussian levels, to capture the image details at each level.

Part 2.4: Multiresolution Blending (a.k.a. the oraple!)

In this section, I used Gaussian and Laplacian image stacks and image splines to blend images together to create hybrid images.

I used masks to control which parts of each image contribute at different locations within the image, with the Gaussian-blurred mask ensuring smooth transitions instead of harsh seams. At each level of the Laplacian stacks, the mask weights image A and its complement weights image B, and then the contributions are summed to reconstruct the blended image.
